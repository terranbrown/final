Oaks

An oak is a tree or shrub in the genus Quercus ([ˈkwɛrkʊs]; Latin "oak tree") of the beech family, Fagaceae. The genus Quercus is native to the Northern Hemisphere, and includes deciduous and evergreen species (such as the blue oak pictured left and the live oak pictured right, respectively) extending from cool temperate to tropical latitudes in the Americas, Asia, Europe, and North Africa. North America contains the largest number of oak species, with approximately 90 occurring in the United States.

California Buckeye

Buckeyes belong to the genus Aesculus (/ˈɛskjʊləs/[1] or /ˈaɪskjʊləs/), and are sometimes called horse chestnuts. All parts of these relatively small trees are toxic, including the large seeds for which they are known. Buckeyes in the foothills are distinguishable from other trees due to their offset seasonal schedule. They drop their leaves, and also green up, earlier than most trees which they are around.

Chaparral

Chaparral (/ˌʃæpəˈræl/) is a shrubland or heathland plant community found primarily in the US state of California and in the northern portion of the Baja California Peninsula, Mexico. Because of its resistance to drought, chaparral covers 5% of the entire state of California, and is shaped by the wildfires which occur annually throughout the state.

Gray Pine

The gray pine, or pinus sabiniana, is a pine endemic to California. It is adapted to long, hot, dry summers and is found in areas with an unusually wide range of precipitation. Gray pines prefer rocky, well drained soil, but also grow in serpentine soil and heavy, poorly drained clay soils. They provide a canopy to the oak trees which grow at similar elevations.
